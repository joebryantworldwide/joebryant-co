I picked up a camera at eighteen. Twenty-one years later, the thing I know
for certain is this: nobody remembers a building. They remember how it felt
to stand inside one.

That feeling is what I photograph.

## The difference between a picture and a frame

A picture documents. A frame *composes* — it decides what the eye meets
first, where the light falls, what stays in shadow. When I walk a property
before a shoot, I'm not looking for angles. I'm looking for the moment the
architecture performs: the hour the sun crosses a travertine wall, the
minute a pool goes still enough to mirror the roofline.

Architects spend years deciding where a window goes. The least a photograph
can do is wait twenty minutes for the light to arrive through it.

## Why interiors are the hardest, and the most honest

Exteriors pose. Interiors confess. A great room tells you exactly what the
designer believed — about proportion, about material, about how people
should move through space. Photographing one is closer to portraiture than
landscape: you're capturing a personality, not a floor plan.

That's why I shoot interiors the way I shoot people. Find the gesture.
Honor the silence around it. Never over-light what was designed to be dim.

## What this means for the people who hire me

Whether it's an architect's portfolio, a $50M listing, or a brand campaign,
the brief is the same: make the viewer *feel the room*. Because the buyer,
the publisher, the client scrolling at midnight — none of them are buying
square footage. They're buying the experience of a space, before they've
ever stood in it.

That's the work. It has been since I was eighteen.

— Joe
